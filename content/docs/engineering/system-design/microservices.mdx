The term microservices refers to an architectural and organizational approach to software development, where a large application is broken into smaller isolated services.  In order to better explain the pros and cons of microservices, let's first look at their counterpart: a monolithic application.

## Monolithic Architecture

In a monolith, the entire application is designed as a single unit, that contains all components of the application. The routing, middlewares, business logic and data access layers are tightly integrated into a single codebase and deployed as a single unit. 

![](/diagram_monolith.png)

**Key Characteristics of Monolithic Architecture:**

1. **Simplicity**: Monolithic architectures are relatively straightforward to develop, deploy, and manage, especially for small to medium-sized applications. This makes it a great choice for lean teams and startups, that are trying to take an app to market quickly.
2. **Tight Coupling**: Components within a monolithic application are tightly coupled, making it easier to navigate and maintain code dependencies.
3. **Scalability Challenges**: Scaling a monolithic application can be challenging, as all components are scaled together. This can lead to inefficiencies and resource wastage. It also makes it harder to build or modify features in parallel as the team is growing.
4. **Limited Technology Flexibility**: Since all components are part of the same codebase, adopting new technologies or languages can be cumbersome and may require significant refactoring.


## Microservices Architecture

When following the principles of a microservice architecture, an application is separated into smaller independent components. Each microservice contains all of the routing, middleware, business logic and database access required to implement one specific feature of the application. To serve a single user request, a microservices-based application can call on many internal microservices to compose its response. Communication between the services usually happens over a network connection through HTTP calls (request/response). Web sockets, message queues, pub/sub, and remote procedure calls (RPC) can also be used to connect standalone services. 

![](/diagram_microservices.png)


**Key Characteristics of Microservice Architecture:**

1. **Service Isolation**: Microservices are designed to be self-contained and focused on a specific feature, allowing for greater isolation and autonomy.
2. **Scalability and Flexibility**: Microservices offer granular scalability, enabling individual components to be scaled independently based on demand. This enhances resource utilization and improves overall performance. It also enables multiple engineers or teams to work on features in parallel more easily and without risking conflicting changes.
3. **Technology Diversity**: Each microservice can be developed using different technologies, languages, and frameworks, providing flexibility to choose the most suitable tools for specific requirements.
4. **Complexity Management**: While microservices offer scalability and flexibility, they introduce complexities related to service discovery, communication, and data consistency. Managing these complexities requires careful architectural design and implementation.


## The Right Choice

The decision between a monolithic and microservice architecture depends on various factors, including the size and complexity of the application, development team size and expertise, scalability requirements and long-term maintenance considerations.

- **Monolithic Architectures** may be suitable for small to medium-sized applications with straightforward requirements, limited scalability needs and a smaller development team.
  
- **Microservice Architectures** is are great choices for large-scale, complex applications with evolving requirements, where scalability, flexibility, and independent deployment of services are crucial.

Generally speaking, for most real life use cases microservices at inception is premature abstraction. Therefore, most microservice architectures emerge through migrations from a monolithic architecture.
